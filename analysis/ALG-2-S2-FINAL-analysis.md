# Complete Multi-Unit Diagnostic Analysis

## Topic: Simplifying Radicals
- Core Mechanism: Factoring radicands using prime factorization to extract perfect $n$-th roots, dealing with variable exponents by dividing the power by the index.
- The Common Operational Trap: Leaving exponents inside the radical that are larger than the index, or failing to realize that $\sqrt{x^{odd}} = x^{even} \cdot x^1$ before dividing the exponent by 2.
- The Golden Rule: **Divide the variable's exponent by the root's index; the quotient comes out as the power, and the remainder stays inside the radical.**

## Topic: Adding and Subtracting Radicals
- Core Mechanism: Combining like radical terms, which requires identical indices and identical radicands.
- The Common Operational Trap: Attempting to add radicands together (e.g., $\sqrt{50} + \sqrt{18} \neq \sqrt{68}$), or incorrectly declaring them "not like terms" before simplifying them completely.
- The Golden Rule: **Always simplify every radical to its prime-factored minimum before attempting to combine coefficients of matching radicands.**

## Topic: Multiplying Binomials with Radicals
- Core Mechanism: Expanding expressions like $(a + \sqrt{b})(c - \sqrt{d})$ using the FOIL method, ensuring numbers multiply numbers and radicals multiply radicals.
- The Common Operational Trap: Forgetting to distribute fully, or incorrectly squaring a binomial like $(a+\sqrt{b})^2$ as $a^2 + b$ instead of generating the middle term $2a\sqrt{b}$.
- The Golden Rule: **Execute the FOIL method systematically, combining integer coefficients outside the radical and multiplying radicands inside the identical root.**

## Topic: Dividing Binomials with Radicals
- Core Mechanism: Eliminating radicals from the denominator by multiplying the numerator and the denominator by the exact conjugate of the binomial denominator.
- The Common Operational Trap: Multiplying only by the radical part instead of the full conjugate, or failing to use parentheses and thus improperly distributing the numerator.
- The Golden Rule: **Always multiply the top and bottom by the conjugate of the denominator, recognizing that $(a+\sqrt{b})(a-\sqrt{b}) = a^2 - b$.**

## Topic: Rational Exponents
- Core Mechanism: Translating expressions between radical form $\sqrt[n]{x^m}$ and exponential form $x^{\frac{m}{n}}$, and applying standard exponent rules.
- The Common Operational Trap: Confusing the numerator and denominator of the fractional exponent, mistakenly placing the power in the denominator and the root in the numerator.
- The Golden Rule: **The fractional exponent is always $\frac{\text{power}}{\text{root}}$—the root is literally at the bottom of the fraction.**

## Topic: Solving Square Root Equations
- Core Mechanism: Isolating the radical expression and squaring both sides to solve for $x$, factoring quadratics if necessary.
- The Common Operational Trap: Failing to check for extraneous solutions after squaring both sides, especially when the original equation equals a variable or a negative value.
- The Golden Rule: **You must plug every calculated solution back into the original, un-squared equation to verify it does not produce a false statement.**

## Topic: Domain of Radical Functions
- Core Mechanism: Finding the set of valid inputs by setting the expression under an even root to be greater than or equal to zero.
- The Common Operational Trap: Ignoring the domain restriction entirely, or forgetting to flip the inequality sign if dividing by a negative number while isolating $x$.
- The Golden Rule: **Set the radicand $\ge 0$ and solve for $x$ to state the domain in interval notation.**

## Topic: Operations with Functions
- Core Mechanism: Adding, subtracting, multiplying, or dividing two defined polynomial functions $f(x)$ and $g(x)$.
- The Common Operational Trap: Failing to distribute the negative sign completely across the second function when subtracting $(f-g)(x) = f(x) - g(x)$.
- The Golden Rule: **Always wrap the substituted function in parentheses to ensure signs are correctly distributed.**

## Topic: Composition of Functions
- Core Mechanism: Evaluating one function inside another, such as $f(g(x))$ or $(f \circ g)(x)$, by substituting the inner function's output into the outer function's input.
- The Common Operational Trap: Evaluating them backwards (doing outer first) or incorrectly expanding a squared binomial when substituting $g(x)$ into an $x^2$ term in $f(x)$.
- The Golden Rule: **Always evaluate from the inside out: find the result of the inner function first, then feed that exact value into the outer function.**

## Topic: Inverse Functions
- Core Mechanism: Finding the inverse $f^{-1}(x)$ by substituting $y$ for $f(x)$, swapping the $x$ and $y$ variables, and solving algebraically for the new $y$.
- The Common Operational Trap: Forgetting to factor out the $y$ variable when it appears in multiple terms (e.g., in rational functions) to isolate it completely.
- The Golden Rule: **Swap $x$ and $y$ immediately, then use inverse operations systematically to isolate $y$, factoring if $y$ appears in two different terms.**

## Topic: Graphing Radical Functions
- Core Mechanism: Transforming the parent graphs $y=\sqrt{x}$ and $y=\sqrt[3]{x}$ using translations $f(x)=a\sqrt{x-h}+k$.
- The Common Operational Trap: Reversing the horizontal shift direction, mistakenly moving right for $+h$ and left for $-h$.
- The Golden Rule: **The horizontal shift is always the opposite sign of what is inside the radical ($x-h$ moves right, $x+h$ moves left).**

## Topic: Solving Exponential Equations
- Core Mechanism: Isolating the exponential base and using logarithms to extract the variable from the exponent position.
- The Common Operational Trap: Taking the logarithm before completely isolating the base and its exponent, such as dividing by the base instead of the coefficient.
- The Golden Rule: **Isolate the exponential term entirely before taking the logarithm of both sides.**

## Topic: Solving Logarithmic Equations
- Core Mechanism: Condensing multiple logarithms using properties of logs, then rewriting the equation in exponential form to solve.
- The Common Operational Trap: Forgetting that the domain of a logarithm is strictly $(0, \infty)$, leading to the acceptance of extraneous solutions that cause a negative argument.
- The Golden Rule: **Always verify that your final $x$ values result in strictly positive arguments for all original logarithms in the equation.**

## Topic: Exponential Growth, Decay, and Compound Interest Applications
- Core Mechanism: Modeling real-world scenarios using $A=P(1 \pm r)^t$, $A=P(1+\frac{r}{n})^{nt}$, or continuous compounding $A=Pe^{rt}$.
- The Common Operational Trap: Inputting the interest rate as a whole number instead of a decimal (e.g., using $4.2$ instead of $0.042$) or confusing the continuous compounding formula with the periodic one.
- The Golden Rule: **Always convert percentage rates to decimals and explicitly identify the compounding frequency ($n$) before selecting the formula.**

## Topic: Simplifying Rational Expressions
- Core Mechanism: Factoring the numerator and denominator completely to cancel identical binomial groups.
- The Common Operational Trap: Illegally canceling individual terms that are separated by addition or subtraction rather than factoring out common products first.
- The Golden Rule: **Never cancel across addition or subtraction; you may only cancel exact matching factors in the numerator and denominator.**

## Topic: Multiplying and Dividing Rational Expressions
- Core Mechanism: Factoring all expressions, converting division into multiplication by flipping the second fraction, and canceling common factors.
- The Common Operational Trap: Forgetting to flip the second fraction before attempting to cross-cancel during division.
- The Golden Rule: **For division, immediately rewrite as multiplication by the reciprocal of the second fraction before factoring and canceling.**

## Topic: Adding and Subtracting Rational Expressions
- Core Mechanism: Finding a Least Common Denominator (LCD) by factoring all denominators, adjusting numerators, and combining.
- The Common Operational Trap: Forgetting to distribute the negative sign to the entire numerator of the second fraction during subtraction.
- The Golden Rule: **Always use parentheses when multiplying numerators to match the LCD, and carefully distribute negative signs when subtracting.**

## Topic: Graphing Rational Functions
- Core Mechanism: Identifying Vertical Asymptotes (denominator zeroes), Holes (cancelled factors), Horizontal Asymptotes (degree comparison), and intercepts.
- The Common Operational Trap: Confusing the rules for Horizontal Asymptotes or failing to plug the hole's $x$-value back into the *reduced* equation to find its $y$-coordinate.
- The Golden Rule: **Determine holes first by finding cancelled factors, then use the completely reduced equation to locate vertical asymptotes and intercepts.**

## Topic: Fundamental Counting Principle (FCP)
- Core Mechanism: Calculating total outcomes by multiplying the number of choices available at each independent stage.
- The Common Operational Trap: Adding the choices together instead of multiplying, or failing to reduce the available options when items cannot be repeated.
- The Golden Rule: **Draw a blank space for every decision or slot, fill in the number of options for each, and multiply them all together.**

## Topic: Permutations
- Core Mechanism: Calculating the number of ways to select and arrange items where the exact order matters.
- The Common Operational Trap: Using combinations instead of permutations, or failing to divide out repeating indistinguishable items in word arrangements.
- The Golden Rule: **If assigning specific distinct roles or ordering items, use $_nP_r$; for word arrangements, divide the total factorial by the factorial of each repeating letter.**

## Topic: Combinations
- Core Mechanism: Calculating the number of ways to select a group where the order is irrelevant and members are treated equally.
- The Common Operational Trap: Misidentifying a scenario as a permutation because of the wording, even though the selected individuals just form a generic subset.
- The Golden Rule: **If the selected items form a unified group with no specific hierarchy or assigned slots, strictly use $_nC_r$.**

## Topic: Basic Probability
- Core Mechanism: Defining probability as the number of desired outcomes divided by the total number of possible outcomes.
- The Common Operational Trap: Calculating based on the wrong total denominator, especially when translating frequency tables without computing the grand total first.
- The Golden Rule: **Always sum the data completely to find your absolute total possible outcomes before calculating any fractional probability.**

## Topic: Probability of Multiple Events
- Core Mechanism: Using multiplication for independent/dependent events (AND) and addition for mutually exclusive/inclusive events (OR).
- The Common Operational Trap: Forgetting to subtract the overlapping probability $P(A \text{ and } B)$ when evaluating non-mutually exclusive OR events.
- The Golden Rule: **For "AND", multiply probabilities; for "OR", add probabilities and always subtract the overlap.**

## Topic: Conditional Probability
- Core Mechanism: Calculating $P(A|B)$, the probability of $A$ occurring given that $B$ has already occurred, by restricting the sample space.
- The Common Operational Trap: Using the grand total as the denominator instead of restricting the denominator to only the given condition's total.
- The Golden Rule: **The "given" condition establishes a new, smaller denominator; you only look at the intersection within that specific row or column.**

## Topic: Binomial Probability
- Core Mechanism: Utilizing the formula $P(r) = _nC_r (p)^r (q)^{n-r}$ for scenarios with fixed trials, independent events, and strictly two outcomes.
- The Common Operational Trap: Misinterpreting "at least" or "at most" constraints and calculating only a single exact success value instead of the sum of the required probabilities.
- The Golden Rule: **Explicitly identify $n$, $r$, $p$, and $q$ first; for cumulative cases ("at most/least"), calculate and sum the probabilities for all valid $r$ values.**

## Topic: Special Right Triangles
- Core Mechanism: Using precise geometric ratios to find exact side lengths in 45-45-90 ($x, x, x\sqrt{2}$) and 30-60-90 ($x, x\sqrt{3}, 2x$) triangles.
- The Common Operational Trap: Multiplying by the radical when you should be dividing to isolate the base leg, or confusing the $\sqrt{2}$ and $\sqrt{3}$ multipliers.
- The Golden Rule: **Always solve for the shortest leg first (the leg opposite the $30^{\circ}$ angle or the base leg in 45-45-90) before finding the other sides.**

## Topic: Right Triangle Trigonometric Ratios
- Core Mechanism: Applying SOH CAH TOA to find missing side lengths when given a reference angle and one side.
- The Common Operational Trap: Setting up the ratio correctly but struggling algebraically when the unknown variable $x$ is in the denominator.
- The Golden Rule: **If $x$ is in the denominator, swap $x$ and the trigonometric function to divide; if $x$ is in the numerator, multiply the denominator by the trig function.**

## Topic: Inverse Trigonometry and Angles of Elevation/Depression
- Core Mechanism: Using $\sin^{-1}, \cos^{-1}, \tan^{-1}$ to calculate missing angles, and translating real-world sightlines into geometric right triangles.
- The Common Operational Trap: Placing the angle of depression incorrectly inside the triangle against the vertical axis instead of measuring it from the horizontal line of sight.
- The Golden Rule: **The angle of depression is always measured downward from the horizontal parallel line, making it alternate interior (and equal) to the angle of elevation.**

## Topic: The Unit Circle
- Core Mechanism: Mapping all standard angles onto a circle of radius 1 to identify their exact $(x, y)$ coordinates, where $x = \cos \theta$ and $y = \sin \theta$.
- The Common Operational Trap: Mixing up the coordinate signs in quadrants II, III, and IV, or swapping the $x$ and $y$ values for the $30^{\circ}$ and $60^{\circ}$ reference angles.
- The Golden Rule: **Always verify the quadrant to determine the correct positive or negative signs for the $(x, y)$ coordinates before stating the final exact value.**

## Topic: Radian Measure and Conversion
- Core Mechanism: Converting angle measurements between degrees and radians using the proportion standard $180^{\circ} = \pi$ radians.
- The Common Operational Trap: Multiplying by the wrong conversion factor and either failing to cancel the $\pi$ or failing to introduce it properly.
- The Golden Rule: **Multiply by $\frac{\pi}{180^{\circ}}$ to convert to radians, and multiply by $\frac{180^{\circ}}{\pi}$ to convert to degrees.**
